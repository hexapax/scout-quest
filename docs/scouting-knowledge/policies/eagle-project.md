The **current Scouting America Eagle Scout service project process** (formerly BSA) follows the Eagle Scout Service Project Workbook (latest edition as of 2023), requiring Scouts to plan, develop, lead, and execute a project while a Life Scout that demonstrates leadership and benefits an organization other than Scouting.[6]

### Project Proposal and Approval Workflow
Scouts must use the most current **Eagle Scout Service Project Workbook** from Scouting.org, completing the Proposal section with details on the project scope, beneficiary, safety, feasibility, and action steps.[1][3][4][6] The workflow includes:
1. Discuss the project idea with **unit leader** (e.g., Scoutmaster), troop Eagle coach, and **project beneficiary** (organization representative) to secure oral approval; provide them "Navigating the Eagle Scout Service Project" from the workbook.[1][2][3]
2. Complete the workbook's Contacts and Proposal pages, including signatures from the **Scout (candidate's promise)**, **beneficiary**, **unit leader**, and **unit committee**.[1][2][3][4][6]
3. Submit the proposal (often in PDF or binder) to the **district advancement chair, Eagle coordinator, or review committee** for approval; processes vary by district (e.g., email submission with 1-week acknowledgment, 1-2 week review via in-person/virtual meeting; allow 5-7 business days minimum, up to 2 months).[1][3][4][7]
4. District committee evaluates against criteria like leadership opportunity, feasibility, safety, and detailed planning; approves in one meeting if possible or provides written feedback for revisions.[3][4][5]
No project work may begin until **district/council approval** is obtained.[1][3]

### Fundraising Rules
If raising funds or seeking material donations from businesses, complete a **fundraising application** in the workbook *before* any activity; obtain approvals from **beneficiary**, **unit leader**, and submit to **council service center**.[1] Leftover funds/materials go to the beneficiary; track all donations by estimated value for the final report.[1]

### Workbook Requirements
- **Proposal**: Describes project, beneficiary role, scope, feasibility, safety, and next steps; must pass tests for leadership opportunity and planning.[5][6]
- **Project Plan** (recommended post-proposal approval): Detailed final plan, developed with a project coach.[3][6]
- Include photos, sketches/diagrams, manpower lists, and material lists as needed.[7]
Track *all* hours: research, planning, meetings, and execution.[1][3]

### Timeline Expectations
Varies by district; proposal review: 5-7 business days to 2 months.[1][3] Schedule ahead for district Eagle boards (monthly in some areas).[2] Overall Eagle process (including project) typically spans months; plan for sufficient lead time before age 18.[3]

### Final Writeup Requirements
Prepare **Project Final Plan**, **Project Report**, and list of project workers in the workbook; include photos, hours, material values, and beneficiary feedback.[1][3] Submit these *with* the Eagle Scout Rank Application to the **council office**.[3] Unit leader and beneficiary must confirm satisfaction (changes allowed if not too significant; approval not required for board if disputed).[5]

### Eagle Scout Application and Reference Letter Requirements
- Submit **Eagle Scout Rank Application** (verified by unit leader) to council with workbook sections (proposal, final plan, report, workers), position/leadership verification, and 6 reference letters (or "to be provided" for 2 from parents/guardians if declined).[3][6][7][8]
- References from: parents/guardians, principal/teacher, employer/coach (if applicable), religious/minister, and 2 others (excluding Scout leaders/relatives); council forwards to district for board.[6]

### Eagle Board of Review Process
After council verifies application and project materials, schedule **Eagle Scout Board of Review** (unit-scheduled initially, district/council for final).[3][4] Board (3-6 members, including unit leader rep) reviews leadership, project execution (not just proposal), Scout spirit, and rank readiness; beneficiary/unit leader input considered but not binding.[3][5] If approved, council issues certificate; no project redo required unless fundamentally flawed.[5] District processes may include video calls with adults present for youth protection.[7]
