### First Class Rank Requirements (Effective January 1, 2025)

The following are the full text of the current **First Class** rank requirements for the Scouts BSA program in Scouting America (formerly BSA), as published in the official Scouts BSA Requirements book effective January 1, 2025. These apply through the 2025-2026 program year. Scouts may work on Scout, Tenderfoot, Second Class, and First Class ranks simultaneously but must earn them in sequence. Alternative requirements exist for Scouts with physical or mental disabilities.[3][4][5]

No major changes to First Class requirements are noted from the 2024 version; updates primarily affect merit badges and higher ranks.[7]

#### CAMPING AND OUTDOOR ETHICS
**1a.** Since joining Scouts BSA, participate in 10 separate troop/patrol activities, at least six of which must be held outdoors. Of the outdoor activities, at least three must include overnight camping. These activities do not include troop/patrol meetings. Any activity, day or overnight, with your patrol or troop can count. Overnight camping must include at least one overnight stay.[3][4][5]  
*Practical tip:* Track activities in a logbook or troop app from day one; prioritize patrols over full troop events for flexibility, and combine with Second Class camping to hit multiples efficiently.

**1b.** On one of these campouts, select your patrol site and return to it three times over a 24-hour period to inspect for hazards and follow the principles of **Leave No Trace** (pages 414–415).[4][5]  
*Practical tip:* During a routine campout, assign patrol members rotating inspection shifts; use the Leave No Trace checklist app for quick documentation.

#### FIRST AID
**2a.** Discuss when a **Scout Oath** and **Scout Law** might be used in first aid and demonstrate that you know how to treat the following:  
• Cuts and scratches  
• Blisters on the hand and foot  
• Minor (thermal/heat) burns or **scalds** (first-degree)  
• Minor (heat-induced) **hypothermia** (situational example)  
• **Heat exhaustion**—prevent and treat  
• **Heatstroke**—prevent and treat  
• **Animal bites or stings** (minor, such as from a small snake, dog, wasp, tick, etc.)  
• **Snakebite**  
• **Torn fingernail** (for example, nail torn halfway off)  
• **Poisonous plants**—know the three plants to avoid and demonstrate/use first aid for exposure (e.g., poison ivy, poison sumac, poison oak).[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Practice on a buddy with safe simulations (e.g., makeup for bites); memorize via flashcards grouping by skin, heat, and bites.

**2b.** Tell what you can do on a troop/patrol outing **to prevent emergencies** based on the principles covered in **First Aid merit badge** requirements.[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Review First Aid merit badge pamphlet during planning meetings; contribute one prevention idea per outing prep.

#### AQUATIC SAFETY (Must Complete **One**)
**3a.** Tell what precautions must be taken for **safe swimming** over deep water. Demonstrate the **feetfirst** jump. (Repeat three times.)[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Use shallow pool edge for jumps if deep water unavailable; video self for review.

**OR**  
**3b.** Swim continuously **100 yards** using a combination of any of the following strokes: **sidestroke**, **breaststroke**, **dog paddle**, or **crawl**; at least 25 yards of the swim using each stroke. Rest as needed.[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Break into 25-yard sets with 30-second rests; train weekly in a pool to build endurance without fatigue.

#### NAVIGATION
**4a.** Explain the importance of using a **map and compass**. Discuss **map symbols** and how to orient a map.[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Use free online topo map tools to practice symbols; orient by lining map edges with landmarks.

**4b.** Discuss **trip planning** and **navigation** by **map and compass** with your patrol leader or other Scouts.[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Prep for next hike by co-planning a 5-mile route with patrol; mark waypoints on map.

**4c.** Using a **map and compass**, complete an **orienteering** course that covers at least **two miles** and requires three legs, at least two of which are more than a half mile. To complete this requirement, you must use **public transportation** to get to the starting point of the course, and you must use **public transportation** to return to the point of origin.[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Join council orienteering events (e.g., First Class events); pre-plot bus routes via apps for urban access.

#### COOKING
**5a.** Plan a menu and **cook one breakfast, one lunch, and one dinner** for your patrol or a similar-sized group. If your troop has a patrol leader’s **mess**, cook for that group instead.[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Choose no-cook breakfast/lunch (e.g., oatmeal, sandwiches) and simple one-pot dinner; shoplist in advance.

**5b.** Using the **menu** planned for First Class requirement **5a**, make a shopping list and **buy the items needed** to prepare **one meal** for your patrol or similar-sized group. Prepare and serve that meal.[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Use troop budget or reimburse; practice solo first to time grocery run under 30 minutes.

#### GENERAL KNOWLEDGE
**6.** Tell how you have lived **four different points of the Scout Law** (different from those used for previous ranks) in your everyday life.[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Journal weekly examples (e.g., "Trustworthy: Returned extra change"); pick fresh points like "Thrifty" or "Brave."

**7.** While working toward the **First Class** rank, and **after completing Second Class requirement 11**, **participate in a Scoutmaster conference**.[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Schedule right after Second Class board; review handbook sections on progress.

**8. Successfully complete your board of review for the First Class rank.**[3][4]  
*Practical tip:* Prepare by quizzing self on all requirements; dress in full uniform and bring handbook.
