### Eagle-Required Status
**Environmental Science is an Eagle-required merit badge.** Scouts must earn **either Environmental Science *or* Sustainability** to qualify for Eagle Scout rank, as one of the 13 required merit badges (out of 21 total).[2][3][5][6] This "or" option has been in place since at least 2013-2014 updates and has not changed recently.[5][6]

### Current Requirements
The following are the **full, current requirements** for the Environmental Science merit badge (effective as of the latest official Scouting America publications, consistent across sources). Scouts using older pamphlets (pre-2013 or earlier revisions) may encounter differences, such as fewer ecology options or no invasive species sub-requirements—always verify with the **most recent merit badge pamphlet** or Scouting.org for your workbook.[5][8]

**1.** Make a timeline of the history of environmental science in America. Identify the contribution made by the Boy Scouts of America (now Scouting America) to environmental science. Include dates, names of people or organizations, and important events.[5]

**2.** Define the following terms: population, community, ecosystem, biosphere, symbiosis, niche, habitat, conservation, threatened species, endangered species, extinction, pollution prevention, brownfield, ozone, watershed, airshed, nonpoint source, hybrid vehicle, fuel cell.[5]

**3.** Do **ONE** activity from **SEVEN** of the following **EIGHT** categories (using the activities in the merit badge pamphlet as the basis for planning and carrying out your projects).[1][5]

   - **Ecology**
     - Conduct an experiment to find out how living things respond to changes in their environments. Discuss your observations with your counselor.
     - Conduct an experiment illustrating the greenhouse effect. Keep a journal of your data and observations. Discuss your conclusions with your counselor.
     - Discuss what an ecosystem is. Tell how it is maintained in nature and how it survives.[5]

   - **Air Pollution** (options typically include experiments on particulates, ozone effects, or acid rain—use pamphlet for full list).[1][5]

   - **Water Pollution** (options include testing water quality, nonpoint sources, or wastewater treatment).[1][5]

   - **Land Pollution** (options cover solid waste, landfills, or soil contamination).[1][5]

   - **Endangered Species** (full options):
     - Do research on one species that was endangered or threatened but which has now recovered. Find out how the organism recovered, and what its new status is. Write a 100-word report on the species and discuss it with your counselor.
     - With your parent's/guardian's and counselor's approval, work with a natural resource professional to identify two projects that have been approved to improve the habitat for a threatened or endangered species in your area. Visit the site of one of these projects and report on what you saw.[5]

   - **Pollution Prevention, Resource Recovery, and Conservation** (options on recycling, energy conservation, or sustainable practices).[1][5]

   - **Hazardous Waste** (options include identifying household hazards or superfund sites).[1][5]

   - **Invasive Species** (full options):
     - Learn to identify the major invasive plant species in your community or camp and explain to your counselor what can be done to either eradicate or control their spread.
     - Do research on two invasive plant or animal species in your community or camp. Find out where the species originated, how they were transported to the United States, their life history, how they are spread, and the recommended means to eradicate or control their spread. Report your research orally or in writing to your counselor.[5]

**4.** Perform the **following** experiments:
   - **a.** Photometer (or light meter) experiment (details in pamphlet: measure light penetration in a forest or urban area).
   - **b.** pH testing of local waters (three samples over time).
   **OR** **4b.** Conduct the common water activity (details in pamphlet).[4][5]
   **Practical tip:** Choose **4a** for efficiency—one site visit vs. three for 4b; complete in an afternoon with a group.[4]

**5.** Using the construction project provided or a plan you create on your own, identify the items that would need to be included in an environmental impact statement for the project planned. Discuss how this applies to your Eagle project if relevant.[1][4][5]
   **Practical tip:** Use a simple local project (e.g., trail build or parking lot); list impacts like habitat loss, erosion, pollution. Great prep for Eagle—research EIS components online or in pamphlet.[1][4]

**6.** Find out about three career opportunities in environmental science. Pick one and find out the education, training, and experience required for this profession. Discuss this with your counselor.[5]
   **Practical tip:** Careers like wildlife biologist, environmental engineer, or forester; use BLS.gov for quick education details (e.g., bachelor's degree common).

**7.** Identify a local environmental problem (e.g., invasive species, pollution); propose a solution and get approval. Carry it out with others, then report results.[5] *(Partial text from sources; full in pamphlet.)*
   **Practical tip:** Tie to requirement 3 (e.g., invasive removal); document with photos and before/after data for counselor meeting.

### Practical Tips for Completion
- **Group work:** Do requirements 3, 4, and 7 at campouts or troop meetings—many options overlap (e.g., ecology experiment during hike).[1][4]
- **Pamphlet essential:** All planning based on official pamphlet activities; older versions lack invasive species or full endangered options.[5]
- **Time estimate:** 1-2 weeks with focus; Eagle scouts often pair with Camping badge for outdoor options.[4]
- **Version note:** Pre-2023 pamphlets match closely, but confirm via Scouting.org hub for any minor 2025 tweaks.[8][9]
