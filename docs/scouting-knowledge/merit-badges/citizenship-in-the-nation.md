**Citizenship in the Nation is one of the 13 Eagle-required merit badges for Scouting America (formerly BSA), with no recent changes to its Eagle-required status.** This badge remains required even after the discontinuance of Citizenship in Society effective February 27, 2026, maintaining Eagle at 13 required plus 8 elective merit badges (total 21).[1][2]

The requirements below reflect the **current 2025-2026 version** (updated in the 2025 Scouting America Requirements, effective January 1, 2026, per the official Merit Badge Hub), which Scouts starting on or after that date must use.[3][4] Scouts who began before January 1, 2026, may finish with the prior version (found in Scoutbook) or switch to this one through December 31, 2026.[4][6] Always verify the latest at www.scouting.org/meritbadges, as pamphlets or handbooks may lag.[3][6] No major changes to this badge are listed in 2026 updates.[5]

### Full Current Requirements with Practical Tips
**1. What is the U.S. Constitution? What are its purposes?**  
*Practical tip:* Read the Preamble and Articles 1-7 online (e.g., archives.gov). Discuss with your counselor how it limits government power; create a one-page summary or mind map for review. Use the merit badge pamphlet for key quotes.

**2. Explain what citizenship in the nation means. Tell how you can demonstrate good citizenship in your community, Scouting unit or troop, school, or your place of worship.**  
*Practical tip:* Define it as rights, duties, and active participation. Give personal examples like voting in student elections or troop service projects; prepare 3-5 specific actions to share verbally.

**3. Name the three branches of U.S. government and explain the role of each one. Pick one current issue and explain how the three branches are responding to it. Name the cabinet-level departments and tell what each one does.**  
*Practical tip:* Branches: Legislative (Congress makes laws), Executive (President enforces), Judicial (Supreme Court interprets). For an issue like climate change, research recent bills, executive orders, and court cases via congress.gov or whitehouse.gov. Memorize 15 departments (e.g., State for diplomacy) with one-sentence roles.

**4. Choose a federal agency that interests you OR a major national historical site. Visit its website OR visit its site (with your parent or guardian’s permission). Then do TWO of the following:**  
**a. Write a short report on what you learned about the agency or site and its role in our nation.**  
**b. Tell your counselor what you learned and explain how the agency or site helps our nation’s citizens.**  
**c. Identify three career opportunities related to the agency or site. Pick one and explain how Scouting has helped you prepare for it.**  
*Practical tip:* Pick engaging options like FBI (fbi.gov) or Statue of Liberty (nps.gov). For careers, link to Eagle projects or leadership roles; visit virtually if travel isn't feasible.

**5. Choose ONE of the following and tell your counselor about it:**  
**a. The U.S. flag—what it is, its history, what it stands for, and how to display it.**  
**b. The national anthem—what the words mean and the history of its origin.**  
**c. The Pledge of Allegiance—its history and meaning.**  
*Practical tip:* Select the flag for visuals; demonstrate proper display (e.g., no beach towels). Practice reciting/explaining to a buddy for confidence.

**6. Name your two U.S. senators and the member of Congress from your congressional district. Write a letter about a national issue and send it to one of these elected officials, sharing your view with him or her. Show or discuss your letter with your counselor.**  
*Practical tip:* Find reps at house.gov/representatives or senate.gov/senators. Pick a timely issue (e.g., environment); keep letter 1 page, polite, with facts. Use certified mail for proof or email via official sites.[7]

**7. Name one national U.S. government program that helps Scouts. Name three other national programs that help people in our nation. While a Scout, take part in ONE of these programs OR view in person or via video a U.S. leader participating in ONE of these programs.**  
*Practical tip:* Scouting examples: Presidential Volunteer Service Awards. Others: SNAP (food), Medicare (health), FEMA (disasters). Attend a volunteer event or watch a presidential speech on YouTube.

**8. Do TWO of the following AND share what you learn with your counselor:**  
**a. Visit a place that is important to our nation’s history OR attend a ceremony or political event related to government or community involvement.**  
**b. Watch a movie or documentary approved by your counselor about a national event that influences Scouts today.**  
**c. Read about an event in our nation’s history that has impacted Scouts OR Scouting. Share what you learned.**  
**d. Learn about a national award and the people who have earned it. Tell how the award was created and why it is important.**  
*Practical tip:* Virtual options work (e.g., Arlington Cemetery tour online). For movies, counselor-preapprove "13th" (civil rights) or "Lincoln." Link to Scouting values like patriotism.

**9. Do ONE of the following:**  
**a. Choose a part of the Constitution OR Bill of Rights important to you. Explain why it is important and how it affects your life. Share your conclusion with your counselor.**  
**b. Pick a phrase or paragraph from the Declaration of Independence that is important to you. Explain why it is important and how it affects your life. Share your conclusion with your counselor.**  
*Practical tip:* 1st Amendment (free speech) is relatable for Scouts. Write 200 words, connect to troop debates or personal expression.

**10. Watch the national evening news on THREE different days. Note three national issues and explain to your counselor how they affect you, your family, and your community.**  
*Practical tip:* Use PBS NewsHour or ABC World News (avoid opinion shows). Track inflation, elections, disasters; discuss local impacts like gas prices.

### Version Differences Scouts Might Encounter
- **Pre-2025 vs. 2025+:** Minor wording clarifications and reorganization for clarity/modern topics (e.g., career links in req. 4c); no major content shifts listed.[3][4][5] Prior version in older pamphlets/Scoutbook emphasizes historical sites more directly.
- **Transition rule:** Pre-2026 starters finish old version; post-January 1, 2026, starters use new.[4][6]
- **Counselor note:** Always confirm with your merit badge counselor which version applies to avoid rework.

Work with a buddy/parent per Youth Protection, get Scoutmaster approval first, and use the official pamphlet.[2] This badge pairs well with Citizenship in the Community/World for Eagle prep.
