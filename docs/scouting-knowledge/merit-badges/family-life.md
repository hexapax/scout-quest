### Eagle-Required Status
**Family Life is an Eagle-required merit badge** for Scouts BSA in Scouting America (formerly BSA). Its Eagle-required status has not changed recently; it remains one of the 13 required badges under the 2025-2026 requirements.[3][5]

### Current Requirements (Effective January 1, 2026)
The requirements were **updated in 2025** (published in Scouting America Requirements 33216), effective January 1, 2026. Scouts starting before this date may finish under the prior version or switch to the new one per Guide to Advancement 7.0.4.3.[3][4] The list below uses the **2026 version** from official sources, with full text where available (some details like req. 6b subtopics are partially excerpted due to source truncation but confirmed consistent).[1][3][7][8][9] Older worksheets or Scoutbook may show pre-2026 versions, so verify with the merit badge pamphlet or Scouting.org.[3]

1. **Prepare an outline on what a family is and discuss this with your merit badge counselor. Tell why families are important to individuals and to society. Discuss how the actions of one member can affect other members.**[1][3][8]  
   *Practical tips:* Use a simple bullet-point outline (e.g., nuclear/extended/blended families). Research family roles via the pamphlet. Practice discussion with parents first; record key points for counselor. Takes 15-30 minutes.

2. **List several reasons why you are important to your family and discuss this with your parents or guardians and with your merit badge counselor.**[1][2]  
   *Practical tips:* Brainstorm 4-6 reasons (e.g., helping with chores, emotional support). Write them down; discuss separately with parents (get signature) and counselor. Submit list/worksheet for verification.[2]

3. **Prepare a list of your regular home duties or chores (at least five) and do them for 90 days. Keep a record of how often you do each of them. Discuss with your counselor the effect your chores had on your family.**[1][3]  
   *Practical tips:* Get counselor pre-approval on list (e.g., dishes, laundry). Use a calendar/app for tracking (aim for 80-100% consistency). Note family feedback in journal. Requires 3 months; start early.[2]

4. **With the approval of your parent or guardian and your counselor, decide on and carry out an individual project that you would do around the home that would benefit your family. After completion, discuss the objective or goal and the results of the project with your family and then your counselor.**[1][3][9]  
   *Practical tips:* Propose simple projects (e.g., organize garage, paint room) for pre-approval. Document before/after photos, costs, time. Discuss challenges/learnings (e.g., responsibility).[2][9] 1-4 weeks typical.

5. **Plan and carry out a project that involves the participation of your family. After completing the project, discuss the following with your merit badge counselor: a. The objective or goal of the project. b. How individual members of your family participated. c. The results of the project.**[1]  
   *Practical tips:* Choose collaborative ideas (e.g., family garden, meal prep week). Assign roles; get approvals. Prepare a 1-page summary/report with photos for counselor review.

6. **Do the following (discussion of each of these subjects may carry over to more than one family meeting):**  
   a. **Discuss with your counselor how to plan and carry out a family meeting.**[1][2]  
      *Practical tips:* Counselor guides on agenda/structuring (10-15 min chat). Focus on facilitation skills.  
   b. **Prepare a meeting agenda that includes the following topics, review it with your parents or guardians, and then carry out one or more family meetings:**  
      1. **The growing-up process and how the body changes, and making responsible decisions dealing with sex. This conversation may take place with only one parent or guardian.**  
      2. *(Source-truncated; typically includes substance abuse/avoiding drugs.)*  
      3. **Personal and family finances.**  
      4. **A crisis situation within your family and who you can turn to for support during these situations.**  
      5. **The effect of technology on your family.**  
      6. **Good etiquette and manners.**[1]  
      *Practical tips:* Split heavy topics over 2-3 meetings (parents lead discussions). Scout prepares agenda, facilitates lightly. Report to counselor what happened (parent sign-off recommended); no need to re-discuss full content with MBC—just confirm occurrence.[1] Topics are parent-led; scout initiates scheduling.[1]

**General tips:** Read the free pamphlet first.[2] Track everything in the official workbook.[7] Projects/chores need pre-approval.[2] For Eagle, emphasize discussions/reflections. Lone Scouts can adapt (e.g., community leadership).[1]
