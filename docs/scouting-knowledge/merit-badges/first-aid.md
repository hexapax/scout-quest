### Eagle-Required Status
**First Aid is a required merit badge for the Eagle Scout rank** and has been since before the 2023 requirements update, with no recent changes to its status[1][2][3][4][5][9]. Scouts must earn it as one of the 13-14 fixed Eagle-required badges (depending on options chosen in other groups)[2][3].

### Current Requirements (Effective January 1, 2025)
The official requirements are listed on Scouting America's Merit Badge Hub and reflect the 2025 pamphlet edition (no major changes from 2023)[9][10]. Older pamphlets (pre-2023) or Scout handbooks may have outdated versions, such as requirement 6c previously including "shock" separately or different phrasing in 1a–1f; always use the current pamphlet or scouing.org for verification[4][6]. Scouts might encounter differences in local troop resources or PDFs from 2023–2024[3][6].

Here are the **full text** of all 13 numbered requirements:

1. **Discuss with your counselor the role and importance of the Boy Scout Handbook and the merit badge pamphlet. Explain how to find items in both. Discuss how the First Aid merit badge and the First Aid requirements for the Tenderfoot, Second Class, and First Class ranks help prepare Scouts to handle emergencies and accidents that might occur on a hike or campout. Demonstrate to your counselor that you have current knowledge of all first-aid requirements for Tenderfoot, Second Class, and First Class ranks.[4][9]**
   - **Practical tips**: Read the handbook sections aloud to your counselor. Practice Tenderfoot 7a/b (cuts, burns), Second Class 6a/b/c/d/e (lost, animal bites, poison, fire), and First Class 7a/b (shock, head/serious injury) demos with a buddy. Use a first-aid kit checklist; do this first as it builds foundation for later requirements.

2. **Using a kit mock-up (or the actual contents from your own first-aid kit), demonstrate and explain the contents and uses of the first-aid kit items listed in the First Aid kit requirements for Second Class and First Class ranks. Have your counselor observe your mock kit and discuss its contents.[9]**
   - **Practical tips**: Build a kit with 20+ items (e.g., bandages, tweezers, soap). Label each and role-play uses like cleaning wounds. Buy a cheap kit from a store and customize; counselors often provide feedback here.

3. **Tell how you would obtain emergency medical assistance from: a. Your home b. A remote location on a wilderness camping trip c. An emergency in a city or on a highway.[4][9]**
   - **Practical tips**: Memorize 911, troop emergency plan, and apps like What3Words for remote spots. Role-play scenarios: home (phone/911), wilderness (SPOT device/SAR), urban (bystanders/AED). Practice with a phone simulator.

4. **Earn the American Red Cross Lifeguarding certification or tell your counselor about your First Aid training from another recognized provider.[9]**
   - **Practical tips**: Red Cross Lifeguarding (5-day course, ~$300) covers CPR/AED; alternatives like Ellis & Associates work. If under 15, explain school/prior training. Schedule early—cert expires 2 years.

5. **Explain the statement “the first aid for any life-threatening situation is to phone for help.” List the six priority elements of “phone for help,” and demonstrate calling for help using a phone mock-up.[9]**
   - **Practical tips**: Priorities: 1) Ensure scene safety, 2) Check responsiveness, 3) Call 911, 4) Exact location, 5) Describe emergency/victim number/condition, 6) Follow instructions. Use a toy phone; record a practice call.

6. **Demonstrate the skills needed for a scout to: a. Handle a cut (Tenderfoot 7a) b. Handle a burn (Tenderfoot 7b) c. Handle a heat exhaustion reaction (Second Class 6c) d. Treat for shock (First Class 7a) e. Treat for a snakebite (Second Class 6d)[9]**
   - **Practical tips**: Use mannequins/bandages for demos. Snakebite: immobilize, clean, no tourniquet/suction. Video yourself practicing; do in sequence as listed.

7. **Do **a. OR b.**: a. Demonstrate bandaging: head, hand/wrist, forearm/elbow, ankle/foot, knee/leg, shoulder/arm. b. For each, explain function and when used.[9]**
   - **Practical tips**: Choose a (hands-on); use partner or dummy with gauze/tape. Practice pressure/triangular bandages. Pressure points only for severe arterial bleeding.

8. **Assemble a personal first-aid kit suitable for a backpacking or 50-mile hike. Explain how each item is used and why included.[9]**
   - **Practical tips**: Lightweight kit (e.g., moleskin for blisters, SAM splint, meds). Weight under 1 lb; justify for wilderness (no quick rescue).

9. **Using your personal first-aid kit, demonstrate: a. Treatment for blisters b. Treatment for sunburn c. Treatment for a sprained ankle d. Treatment for heat cramps e. Treatment for cold (hypothermia) exposure.[9]**
   - **Practical tips**: Blisters: protect/drain if needed; hypothermia: shelter/rewarm slowly. Use ice packs/props; tie to kit from #8.

10. **Describe poisons, venoms, toxins; symptoms/treatments for inhaled, absorbed, ingested. List antidotes if any. Show cleaning/treating poison ivy rash.[9]**
    - **Practical tips**: Inhaled (fresh air), absorbed (flush), ingested (do not induce vomit—call poison control 1-800-222-1222). Rash: soap/water, calamine. Use pictures/models.

11. **From simulations: a. Lost person b. Serious open wound c. Serious closed wound d. Heart attack/stroke e. Unconscious/skull fracture f. Broken bone (upper arm, forearm, lower leg).[9]**
    - **Practical tips**: Group skits with troop; use makeup for wounds. Heart attack: aspirin/CPR if no pulse; fractures: splint/immobilize.

12. **Tell how to inspect vehicle for emergencies; contents of highway kit. Describe actions for: a. Traffic accident b. Car breakdown.[9]**
    - **Practical tips**: Kit: flares, blanket, water. Accident: safety first, aid victims, call 911. Breakdown: triangles, safe spot.

13. **Do **two** of: a. Take Red Cross First Aid: CPR/AED b. Mult media training (e.g., Ellis) c. Wilderness first aid course d. Home NACES narcans e. Standard first aid (adult).[9]**
    - **Practical tips**: Easiest: a or b (online options). Get signed card; combine with #4 if possible.

### Additional Notes
- Complete with a registered merit badge counselor (often troop First Aid chair or Red Cross instructor)[4][9].
- Workbook helps track; sign off as you go[10].
- For Eagle, pair with Camping/Emergency Prep for overlap[7].
- Requirements updated Jan 1, 2025; confirm on scouting.org if pamphlet differs[9][10].
