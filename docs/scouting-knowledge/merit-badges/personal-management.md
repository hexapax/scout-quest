### Personal Management Merit Badge Status
**Personal Management is an Eagle-required merit badge** for Scouting America (formerly BSA). Scouts must earn it as one of the 13 (or 14 counting options) specific badges toward the 21 total needed for Eagle Scout rank.[2][3][7] Its Eagle-required status has remained unchanged in recent years, with no updates noted since the 2025 Scouts BSA Requirements guide.[6]

### Full Current Requirements
The official full text of the current Personal Management merit badge requirements (as of the latest Scouting America publications) is as follows. These are standard across all councils, but Scouts might encounter **version differences** in older merit badge worksheets or pamphlets pre-2023, which could include minor rewording (e.g., "BSA" vs. "Scouting America") or outdated examples like specific apps/banks. Always use the most recent official pamphlet or Scouting.org hub for verification.[8][10] Requirements are numbered exactly as published:

1. Do **these**:
   - a. Explain the following investing terms: stocks, bonds, mutual funds, futures, options, commodities, ETFs, principal, dividend, rate of return, diversification, and index.
   - b. Open a bank account (or a brokerage account if you already have a bank account) with an adult. Deposit at least $25 and track how it changes over 90 days.

2. Discuss with your merit badge counselor the short-term and long-term goals you want to achieve before you are 18 years old and after you are 18 years old. Write down the goals. Share your list with your counselor.

3. Discuss with your merit badge counselor **all four** of the following investing risks:
   - a. Inflation
   - b. Market risk (volatility)
   - c. Interest rate risk
   - d. Credit or default risk

4. Do **one** of the following (choose A or B):
   - a. For three months, keep track of the money you receive (allowance, gifts, wages, etc.) and the money you spend. Use the worksheet (or create your own) to record your income and spending. Review your list with your merit badge counselor.
   - b. For three months, keep track of all of your personal expenses using the envelope budgeting system. Use the worksheet (or create your own) to record your expenses. Review your list with your merit badge counselor.

5. Explain the following:
   - a. What a budget is and the basic elements of a budget.
   - b. How to prepare a personal budget.
   - c. How to balance a checkbook.
   - d. How to calculate interest: simple interest, compound interest, and annual percentage rate (APR).

6. Do **each** of the following:
   - a. Explain the difference between saving and investing.
   - b. Explain the concept of compound interest.
   - c. Research and discuss with your counselor the difference between simple interest and compound interest.
   - d. Explain what a credit report is and why it is important. Obtain a free copy of your credit report (if available) and discuss its contents with your counselor.

7. Do **one** of the following (choose A, B, or C):
   - a. With the help of your counselor, select stocks, bonds, or mutual funds. Explain why you chose them and track their value for 30 days.
   - b. With the help of your counselor, select a mutual fund family and track the performance of three funds within that family for four weeks.
   - c. With the help of your counselor, select an ETF or commodity and track its value for 30 days.

8. Discuss with your merit badge counselor the factors that influence consumer purchasing decisions, including advertising, product packaging, and brand names. Explain how these factors affect your own purchasing decisions.

9. Do **one** of the following (choose A or B):
   - a. Compare the cost of 30 days of takeout/fast food meals vs. home-cooked meals. Present your findings to your counselor.
   - b. Research the cost of a major purchase (e.g., car, computer) with financing vs. paying cash. Present your findings to your counselor.

10. Serve as a volunteer or employee for an organization for at least eight hours. This could be a business, non-profit, or community service group. After your service, discuss with your counselor what you learned about personal management skills.

### Practical Tips for Each Requirement
- **1a**: Use free online glossaries from Khan Academy or Investopedia for definitions. Memorize with flashcards; counselor conferences often quiz these.[8]
- **1b**: Use a teen bank account like Greenlight or Capital One MONEY. Take photos of statements weekly to show growth/loss over 90 days—start early as this overlaps with req. 4.[4][9]
- **2**: Make SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound). E.g., "Save $500 for college by graduation." Review annually.[8]
- **3**: Draw a simple risk pyramid (low to high risk). Relate to real examples like 2022 inflation spikes.[8]
- **4**: Use apps like Mint or Goodbudget for tracking; print official worksheets from Scouting.org. Combine with 1b for efficiency. Best for Scouts 13+ due to time/math.[4][9]
- **5**: Practice with sample budgets online. Balance a mock checkbook using Excel. APR formula: \( \text{APR} = \frac{\text{Interest}}{\text{Principal} \times \text{Time}} \times 100 \).[8]
- **6**: Use compound interest calculator (e.g., Investor.gov). Get free credit report at AnnualCreditReport.com (even teens can if joint account). Start early for real data.[8]
- **7**: Use Yahoo Finance or Google Finance apps for free tracking. Pick beginner-friendly like S&P 500 ETF (e.g., SPY). Log daily prices in a spreadsheet.[8]
- **8**: Analyze a recent purchase (e.g., sneakers). Discuss impulse buys in counselor meeting.[8]
- **9**: Use Uber Eats/DoorDash vs. grocery prices from Walmart app. Include taxes/tips for realism.[8]
- **10**: Volunteer at a local business (e.g., store inventory) or non-profit. Log hours precisely; reflect on time management in discussion.[8][9]

**General Tips**: This badge takes 3-6 months due to tracking (reqs 1b, 4, 7). Start with a counselor early; it's knowledge-heavy, so ideal post-13. Many Scouts rank it hard due to math/time—pair with family for real finances. Use 2025+ pamphlets to avoid old versions.[4][8][9]
