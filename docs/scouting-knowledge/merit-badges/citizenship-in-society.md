**The Citizenship in Society merit badge has been discontinued by Scouting America effective February 27, 2026.** As of that date, no new Scouts can start its requirements, though those who completed at least one requirement before February 27, 2026, may finish by December 31, 2026, with it counting toward Eagle Scout for eligible Life Scouts.[1][2][3][5]

### Eagle-Required Status and Recent Changes
- It **was Eagle-required** starting July 1, 2022, as one of 14 required merit badges (requiring 7 total from the list).[4]
- **Recent change**: Discontinued February 27, 2026, reducing Eagle to **13 required merit badges** (with 8 electives instead of 7, totaling 21 merit badges). This is a reversal from its addition as a new Eagle-required badge.[1][2][5]
- Grace period applies only to those who started pre-discontinuance; others cannot earn it post-February 27, 2026.[2][3]

### Full Text of Requirements
Search results do not provide the full official text of the requirements (the 2025 Scouts BSA Requirements PDF excerpt in [6] covers ranks, not this merit badge; official details were at scouting.org/meritbadges pre-discontinuance).[5][6] Scouts should reference archived merit badge pamphlets or scouting.org (valid through 2025) for exact wording. Requirements emphasized facilitated discussions on diversity, equity, inclusion, and ethical leadership, unlike traditional merit badges.[4]

**Note on version differences**: No major revisions noted in results up to 2026; it used a single set since 2022. Scouts starting pre-2026 use those; post-discontinuance, unavailable. 2025 requirements book (effective Jan 1, 2025) matched pamphlets, with annual updates possible but none specified here.[6]

### Practical Tips for Completion (for Eligible Scouts)
Since full requirements aren't in results, tips are based on described focus (discussion-based, not lecture).[4] Complete promptly if eligible, before Dec 31, 2026.

- **Preparation**: Review Scout Oath/Law for diversity/leadership ties. Complete online DEI training if facilitating (though Scouts discuss, not lead).[4]
- **Discussions/Interviews**: Practice active listening; journal personal experiences with inclusion. Interview diverse peers/adults safely (virtual OK). Tip: Record notes immediately; use examples from school/troop.
- **Research**: Watch short videos/news on equity topics. Tip: Cite 2-3 sources per topic; discuss biases factually.
- **Service Project**: Plan small, local action (e.g., inclusive troop event). Tip: Get counselor pre-approval; document impact with photos/notes.
- **Facilitator Sessions**: Find approved counselor via council (e.g., Atlanta requires MBC form).[4] Tip: Attend full sessions; participate openly—it's group-led.
- **Reflection**: Write honest essay on learnings. Tip: Connect to everyday Scouting (e.g., troop inclusivity).
- **General**: Track progress in handbook; switch versions only within calendar year if updated (per policy).[6] Verify with advancement chair for Eagle credit.[1][2]
