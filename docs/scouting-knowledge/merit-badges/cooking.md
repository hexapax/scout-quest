### Eagle-Required Status
The **Cooking merit badge** is **Eagle-required** for Scouting America (formerly BSA) and has been since its revision in 2014; no recent changes as of 2026.[1][2][4][6]

### Current Requirements (2026 Version)
The following lists the **full text** of all 11 requirements from the official 2026 Cooking merit badge pamphlet. Meals for requirements 4, 5, and 6 count **only** toward this badge (not rank or other badges), and no menus may repeat across them.[1][2][3][5]

1. **Do the following:**
   - a. Explain to your counselor the most likely hazards you might encounter while participating in cooking activities and what you should do to anticipate, help prevent, mitigate, and respond to these hazards. Name and explain the purpose of the tools and equipment needed to prepare recipes in this merit badge.
   - b. Explain to your counselor why the following are important: (1) to keep a sanitary work area, (2) to prevent cross-contamination between different types of food, (3) to keep perishable ingredients refrigerated, (4) to wash your hands before handling food, and (5) to avoid contact between uncooked and cooked meat or poultry.
   - c. When cooking, name and explain at least **10 cooking methods** (such as baking, boiling, pan frying, stewing, stir frying, deep frying, foil cooking, skewer cooking, etc.) and **five cooking terms** (such as basting, blanching, braising, broiling, creaming, deglazing, flambé, marinating, parboiling, poaching, reducing, roasting, sautéing, simmering, steaming, sweating, etc.). Give examples of how to use each method and term.[1][2]

2. **Using your knowledge from requirement 1, plan **four complete meals** (two breakfasts, one lunch, one dinner) for your patrol or a group of youth totaling four or more persons (including yourself). These four meals must include two breakfasts, one lunch, and one dinner. Additionally, you must plan one snack and one dessert. Your menus should include enough food for each person, keeping in mind any special needs (such as food allergies) and how you keep your foods safe and free from cross-contamination. List the equipment and utensils needed to prepare and serve these meals.** Adjust menu items in the recipes for the number to be served. Create a shopping list and budget to determine the per-person cost. Share and discuss your menu plans and shopping list with your counselor.[1][2][6]

3. **Describe how meat, fish, chicken, eggs, dairy products, and fresh vegetables should be stored, transported, and properly prepared for cooking. Explain how to prevent cross-contamination.** Discuss your current eating habits with your counselor and what you can do to eat healthier, based on the **MyPlate food guide**. Discuss with your counselor why reading food labels is important. Explain how to identify common allergens such as peanuts, tree nuts, milk, eggs, wheat, soy, and shellfish.[1]

4. **Using at least five of the 10 cooking methods from requirement 3, prepare and serve yourself and at least one adult (parent, family member, guardian, or other responsible adult) one breakfast, one lunch, one dinner, and one dessert from the meals you planned. The meals for requirement 4 may be prepared on different days, and they need not be prepared consecutively. Those served need not be the same for all meals.** Time your cooking to have each meal ready to serve at the proper time. Have an adult verify the preparation of the meal to your counselor. After each meal, ask a person you served to evaluate the meal on presentation and taste, then evaluate your own meal. Discuss what you learned with your counselor, including any adjustments that could have improved or enhanced your meals. Tell how planning and preparation help ensure a successful meal.[1][2]

5. **In the outdoors, using your menu plans and recipes for this requirement, cook two of the four meals you planned using either a camp stove OR backpacking stove. Use a skillet OR a Dutch oven over campfire coals for the third meal, and cook the fourth meal in a foil pack OR on a skewer. Serve all of these meals to your patrol or a group of youth.** In the outdoors, using your menu plans and recipes for this requirement, prepare one snack and one dessert. Serve both of these to your patrol or a group of youth. After each meal, have those you served evaluate the meal on presentation and taste, and then evaluate your own meal. Discuss what you learned with your counselor, including any adjustments that could have improved or enhanced your meals. Tell how planning and preparation help ensure successful outdoor cooking. Lead the clean-up of equipment, utensils, and the cooking site thoroughly after each meal. Properly store or dispose unused ingredients, leftover food, dishwater and garbage. Discuss how you followed the **Leave No Trace Seven Principles** and the **Outdoor Code** when preparing your meals.[1][2]

6. **While on a trail hike or backpacking trip, prepare and serve two meals and a snack from the menu planned for this requirement. At least one of those meals must be cooked over a fire, or an approved trail stove (with proper supervision).** After each meal, have those you served evaluate the meal on presentation and taste, then evaluate your own meal.[1][2]

7. **Discuss with your counselor the benefits and importance of good nutrition and balanced diets. Explain the dangers of poor nutrition and sedentary lifestyles. Explain why reading food labels is important. Explain how to identify common allergens such as peanuts, tree nuts, milk, eggs, wheat, soy, and shellfish.** Track your daily level of activity and your daily caloric need based on your activity for five days. Then, based on the **MyPlate food guide**, discuss with your counselor an appropriate meal plan for yourself for one day.[1][2]

8. **Plan a meal for a patrol or group of youth to be prepared on a backpacking trip covering at least four hours of hiking. Include the equipment and utensils needed to prepare and serve this meal.** Share and discuss your menu plans and shopping list with your counselor.[2]

9. **Explain the use of an ice chest for keeping foods safe. Explain how to keep the ice chest sanitary. Tell how to transport ice chest and food without damaging it or contaminating the food.**[1]

10. **Demonstrate proper care and storage of your camp stove, fuel, and utensils. Explain how to safely use and maintain your camp stove.** Discuss the benefits of using a camp stove on an outing vs. a charcoal or wood fire. Describe for your counselor how to manage your time when preparing a meal so components for each course are ready to serve at the correct time. Explain and give examples of how taste, texture, and smell impact what we eat.[1][2]

11. **Explore careers related to this merit badge. Research one career to learn about the training and education needed, costs, job prospects, salary, job duties, and opportunities in your area.** This requirement is new as of 1/1/2026.[7]

### Practical Tips for Each Requirement
- **1**: Practice demos with counselor first. Hazards include burns/cuts—use gloves, tongs. Cooking methods: e.g., **baking** (oven), **stir-frying** (wok). Tip: Film a video explanation if counselor unavailable.[1][2]
- **2**: Use apps for shopping lists/budgets. Scale recipes (e.g., double for 4 people). Account for allergies—label plans. Tip: Get pre-approval to avoid rework.[1][2]
- **3**: MyPlate: half plate fruits/veggies. Store meat below 40°F. Tip: Use color-coded boards (raw meat red, veggies green) for cross-contamination.[1]
- **4**: Cook on separate days; video for verification. Use timers. "Cooking" means transforming food (no Pop-Tarts/hotdogs alone). Tip: Practice timing—prep mise en place first.[1][2]
- **5**: Minimum group: patrol/youth (divide large patrols). Different methods per meal. Clean-up: strain dishwater, pack out trash. Tip: Practice fires safely; LNT means scatter gray water.[1][2]
- **6**: Hike ≥4 hours; one meal over fire/stove supervised. Tip: Lightweight gear; test pack weight pre-hike.[1][2]
- **7**: Log activity via app (e.g., steps/calories). Allergens: "Contains" bold on labels. Tip: Compare your diet to MyPlate visually.[1][2]
- **8**: Focus on lightweight, no-cook options possible. Tip: Dehydrated foods save weight.[2]
- **9**: Ice lasts 2-3 days; drain meltwater. Tip: Pre-freeze foods as ice packs.[1]
- **10**: Store fuel upright, away from tent. Tip: Time management: list tasks backward from serve time.[1][2]
- **11**: Careers: chef, dietitian. Use BLS.gov for data. Tip: Interview local pro.[7]

### Version Differences Scouts Might Encounter
Older worksheets/pamphlets (pre-2026) may lack req. 11 (careers) or have minor wording (e.g., "Boy Scouts" vs. "Scouting America"). Always use **2026 pamphlet/worksheet**; pre-blue card meals don't count retroactively. No major changes since 2022 per sources.[1][3][5][7] Confirm with counselor/official site for latest.
