**Citizenship in the World is an Eagle-required merit badge.** It remains one of the 13 required merit badges for Eagle Scout as of March 2026, following the discontinuance of Citizenship in Society effective February 27, 2026 (previously 14 required; now 13 required + 8 electives = 21 total).[1][2][3][4] No recent changes affect its Eagle status.[4]

The full current requirements (effective as of the 2025-2026 merit badge guidebook, per Scouting America's official hub) are listed below with exact text, practical tips, and notes on version differences. Scouts may encounter older pamphlets (pre-2024 revisions) with minor wording changes, such as expanded global examples or updated discussion prompts, but core requirements are stable; always use the latest pamphlet or official hub for sign-off.[7]

1. **Explain what citizenship in the world means to you, and what you think it means to be a good citizen of your country. Explain how you think your good citizenship helps others in your country and the world.**  
   *Practical tips:* Start with a personal reflection (e.g., journal or video 1-2 minutes). Discuss rights/duties like voting or volunteering. Share with your counselor via essay or discussion. Use examples like helping immigrants or global aid.  
   *Version note:* Pre-2024 versions emphasized patriotism; current adds global interconnectedness.

2. **Describe the role that governmental bodies (local, state/province, national) and nongovernmental organizations play in your community, nation, and the world.**  
   *Practical tips:* Research via websites (e.g., UN, Red Cross, local mayor's office). Create a chart comparing roles (e.g., FEMA for disasters nationally). Interview a local official.  
   *Version note:* Consistent, but recent pamphlets include NGOs like Amnesty International.

3. **Do TWO of the following:**  
   a. **Visit the Web site (With your parent or guardian’s permission) of an organization such as the United Nations, International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC), or Amnesty International. Find out what these organizations have been doing to aid nations struck by natural disasters. Make a report to your counselor about ONE of these incidents. Include a description of the destruction caused and the relief provided.**  
      *Practical tips:* Use un.org or icrc.org; pick a recent event like a 2025 earthquake. Summarize in a 1-page report with photos (describe if no printer).  
   b. **At a meeting of your city council, school board, or other local governmental body, witness how a proposed law is debated and then passed or rejected. Write a report on what you learned.**  
      *Practical tips:* Check city calendars; attend virtually if allowed. Note pros/cons debated.  
   c. **Read about an incident where a law was debated and passed or rejected. Explain why it was proposed and what its effects were (or would have been).**  
      *Practical tips:* Use news sites for examples like international treaties (e.g., Paris Agreement).  
   d. **Choose ONE of the following: (1) the United Nations. (2) an international organization such as the International Committee of the Red Cross. (3) an nongovernmental organization working to help nations strike by natural disasters. Research its charter and cite THREE ways it has helped promote peace, understanding, or goodwill among nations. Make a report to your counselor.**  
      *Practical tips:* Focus on UN peacekeeping; cite specific missions.  
   *Version note:* Option d added in recent revisions for flexibility.

4. **Attend and observe either a city/town council meeting or a court session. Afterward, write a report on what you observed, including how laws are made or justice administered.**  
   *Practical tips:* Find public sessions (many stream online). Note procedures, key arguments. Submit a 300-word summary.

5. **Choose TWO of the following and make a report to your counselor on each one:**  
   a. **The United Nations**  
   b. **Interpol**  
   c. **European Union**  
   d. **World Trade Organization**  
   e. **North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO)**  
   f. **Organization of American States**  
   g. **International Court of Justice (World Court)**  
   *Practical tips:* Pick familiar ones (e.g., UN, NATO). Report structure: purpose, members, recent actions (1 page each). Use official sites.

6. **Do TWO of the following and share what you learn with your counselor:**  
   a. **Visit, with your parent or guardian’s permission, the Web site for one of the following organizations and explain to your counselor what the organization does and how it helps the world: UNICEF, World Health Organization (WHO), or International Monetary Fund (IMF).**  
      *Practical tips:* UNICEF focuses on kids; summarize programs.  
   b. **While on a trip, make a journal of the customs and cultural differences you observe. Share your journal with your counselor.**  
      *Practical tips:* Use family vacation; note food, greetings.  
   c. **Describe the characteristics of a culture or ethnic group different from your own. Share what you learned.**  
      *Practical tips:* Interview someone or research (e.g., Hmong Americans).  
   d. **Describe a national or international event and how it affects you personally. Explain how it has affected others around the world.**  
      *Practical tips:* Tie to current events like climate summits.  
   e. **Research a migratory bird or endangered animal. Explain its migration route and why it is endangered. Share what you learned.**  
      *Practical tips:* Monarch butterfly example.  
   f. **With your counselor’s and parent or guardian’s approval, contact an embassy or consulate official from another country and discuss the role of that country’s representatives in the United States.**  
      *Practical tips:* Email template: polite inquiry on diplomacy.  
   *Version note:* Expanded options in 2024 for inclusivity.

7. **Watch news coverage (print, television, or Internet) of a current international event. Keep a log for one week and discuss with your counselor how the event affects you, your family, and the world.**  
   *Practical tips:* Track one story (e.g., trade war). Log daily impacts; discuss biases in coverage.

8. **Identify an organization in your community that helps internationally (e.g., Doctors Without Borders). Volunteer or donate to it and report your experience.**  
   *Practical tips:* Local chapters of global charities; 2-4 hours service. Document with photos/log.

9. **Exchange a letter, email, or greeting card with a Scout in another country (with counselor/parent approval). Learn about their Scouting and country.**  
   *Practical tips:* Use Scouting networks or JOTA; share badges/customs.

10. **Participate in a World Scouting event or activity. Describe your experience.**  
    *Practical tips:* Jamboree on the Air (JOTA) or Messengers of Peace project.

**General tips:** Schedule with a registered counselor early; complete discussions first as they inform later requirements. Use the official Scouting America app/pamphlet for worksheets. Track progress in Scoutbook. For Eagle, pair with other citizenship badges.[1][3][7]
