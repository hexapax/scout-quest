## What I Can Confirm

**Citizenship in the Community is NOT Eagle-required.**[5] However, it's important to distinguish this from **Citizenship in Society**, which was recently discontinued. The Citizenship in Society Merit Badge was discontinued effective February 27, 2026, and is no longer available to new Scouts—though Life Scouts who started it before that date may complete it by December 31, 2026 for Eagle credit.[1][2]

## Available Requirements Information

The search results confirm that Citizenship in the Community was recently updated in 2025 Scouting America Requirements (33216), with changes effective in 2026.[5] The search results provide partial requirement details:

**Requirement 1:** Discuss with your counselor what citizenship in the community means and what it takes to be a good citizen in your community. Discuss the rights, duties, and obligations of citizenship, and explain how you can demonstrate good citizenship in your community, Scouting unit, place of worship, or school.[4]

**Requirement 2:** Using an electronic mapping tool or paper map, locate and pinpoint the following services and landmarks in your community. Determine and record the distances from your home including driving time AND either walking or biking time:
- Chief government buildings such as your city hall, county courthouse, and public works/services facilities[4]
- Fire station, police station, and hospital nearest your home[3]
- Parks, playgrounds, recreation areas, and trails[3]
- Historical or other interesting points of interest[3]

**Requirement 7:** Includes a volunteer component requiring at least eight hours of service with a charitable organization of your choice.[3][4]

## Practical Tips

- Use **Google Maps** to bookmark all required locations and automatically calculate driving and walking/biking times.[3]
- **Prepare answers beforehand** before meeting with your counselor about your volunteer experience.[3]
- Connect your work on local issues (typically requirement 4) with your volunteer service (requirement 7) by choosing an organization addressing an issue that affects you personally.[5]
- When volunteering, track your responsibilities and service hours, and notice which tasks you enjoyed—this information is valuable for future career planning.[3]
