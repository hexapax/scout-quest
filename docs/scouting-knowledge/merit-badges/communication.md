### Communication Merit Badge Status
The **Communication merit badge** is required for the Eagle Scout rank, one of 14 specific Eagle-required merit badges (with options in some groups, such as Emergency Preparedness or Lifesaving).[1][4][5][6] Its Eagle-required status has not changed recently; it remains a core requirement as of 2025-2026, listed consistently across official Scouting America resources and Eagle trackers.[2][3][4][5][6]

**Note on versions:** Scouts may encounter slight differences in older merit badge pamphlets or worksheets predating 2025 updates—always verify the latest requirements via scouting.org/merit-badges/communication or Scoutbook, as pamphlets direct users there for current text.[8][9] The requirements below reflect the official current version from Scouting America's Merit Badge Hub.[2]

### Full Current Requirements with Practical Tips
Here is the complete list of requirements, including **full text** (sourced from the official hub and pamphlet). Practical tips are tailored for completion, drawing from counselor experiences and overviews emphasizing hands-on practice like speeches and group sessions.[1][2][3]

1. **For each of the following four skills, do **A** OR **B**:**  
   **A. Speaking**  
   (1) Think of a creative 30-second commercial about your favorite product or service. Record it on your phone or camera, and then watch it, noting your strengths and areas to improve.  
   **B. Writing**  
   (1) Write a 300-word humorous essay about something that has happened in your life. Share it with your merit badge counselor or another adult.  
   **Practical tip:** Start with speaking if you're outgoing—practice your commercial 5-10 times for natural delivery; use a timer app. For writing, outline first (setup, punchline, resolution) to hit word count easily. Get counselor pre-approval to ensure it meets creativity standards.[1][2][3]

2. **Deliver a 5-minute speech to your troop or another group approved by your counselor.**  
   **Practical tip:** Choose a topic you're passionate about (e.g., Scouting adventure). Script it fully, time rehearsals, and practice eye contact/body language. Present at a troop meeting for low-pressure feedback; counselors often group sessions for this.[1][2]

3. **Interview someone you know fairly well (friend, family, or fellow Scout). Record the interview (audio or video). Share your recording with your counselor.**  
   **Practical tip:** Prepare 5-10 open-ended questions (e.g., "What challenges shaped your career?"). Get permission first, listen actively without interrupting, and review for clarity. Use free apps like Voice Recorder; aim for 10-15 minutes total.[2]

4. **Attend at least one public meeting (city council, school board, Scout council executive committee, or public meeting by a person you want to interview for requirement 5). After the meeting, do the following:**  
   (1) Write 250 words about the purpose of the meeting and what you learned.  
   (2) Tell your counselor within a week after the meeting how it related to the topics in this merit badge.  
   **Practical tip:** Check local calendars online; attend virtually if allowed. Take notes on speakers' structure and audience interaction. Tie it back to communication skills like persuasion—submit notes promptly for credit.[1][2]

5. **Interview an adult (unrelated to requirement 3) you have not previously interviewed. Prepare for and introduce them as a guest speaker to your troop or another group approved by your counselor. The guest speaker should speak for at least 5 minutes.**  
   **Practical tip:** Pick a professional (e.g., teacher, firefighter); email questions ahead. Write a 1-minute intro highlighting their expertise. Rehearse the handover; coordinate with your SPL for troop meeting slot.[1][2][3]

6. **Plan a troop court of honor, campfire program, or an Adventurer Club or Pathfinder Club event or an event for another youth group approved by your counselor. Serve as master of ceremonies (MC).**  
   **Practical tip:** Volunteer for an upcoming troop event; create a script with timings, transitions, and fun elements (e.g., skits). Practice pacing to keep it engaging—get SPL buy-in early.[1][2]

7. **Plan and teach a 30-minute class or workshop on a topic approved by your counselor to younger Scouts or members of your community.**  
   **Practical tip:** Choose something simple like "Knots for Everyday Use"; use visuals/slides. Practice with a peer first, time it strictly, and end with Q&A. Pre-approve topic with counselor.[3]

8. **Give a short sales presentation about a product or service to your troop, patrol, or another group approved by your counselor.**  
   **Practical tip:** Sell something Scout-related (e.g., camping gear). Use AIDA (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action) structure; demo the product. Keep it 3-5 minutes with enthusiasm.[1]

9. **Find out about three career opportunities in communication. Pick one and find out the education, training, and experience required for this profession. Discuss this with your counselor.**  
   **Practical tip:** Research roles like journalist, public relations specialist, or podcaster via sites like BLS.gov. Prepare notes on entry-level paths (e.g., degree + internship). Discuss verbally for depth.[7]

**Completion notes:** This badge is hands-on and often completed late due to public speaking demands (rated difficulty 8/10).[1] Work with a counselor early for approvals (reqs 1,4,5,7,8); group sessions at libraries boost efficiency.[2] Track progress in Scoutbook.[8][9]
