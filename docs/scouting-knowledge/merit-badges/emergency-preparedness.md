**Emergency Preparedness is an Eagle-required merit badge.** Scouts must earn either **Emergency Preparedness** or **Lifesaving** (one of the 13 Eagle-required merit badges out of 21 total needed).[1][2][3][6] Its Eagle-required status has remained unchanged since it became optional in place of Lifesaving around 1990 (no recent changes noted as of 2025).[5]

The full current requirements (effective as of the latest official Scouting America resources, with no major updates indicated for 2025)[7][10] are listed below with exact text, practical tips for completion, and notes on version differences. Requirements are hands-on and emphasize planning; scouts might encounter pre-2025 versions (e.g., minor wording tweaks in 2024 or earlier) in older worksheets, but core content is stable—always use the current official pamphlet or Scouting.org.[7]

1. **Earn the First Aid merit badge.**
   - *Practical tip:* Complete this first as it's foundational; review bandaging, CPR, and shock treatment. If already earned for Eagle, discuss how it applies to emergencies with your counselor.
   - *Version note:* Unchanged.

2. **Do the following:**
   - **2a.** Discuss the aspects of emergency preparedness:
     - **2a1.** Being informed and prepared in case of a disaster
     - **2a2.** Family disaster plans
     - **2a3.** The appropriate role for a Scout in a family emergency plan
   - *Practical tip:* Research local risks (e.g., floods, earthquakes) via FEMA.gov or ready.gov; create a family plan template and present it. Involve family for realism.
   
   - **2b.** Using a simple kit such as the one listed in the discussion for requirement 2a1 or 8a, demonstrate preparing a family kit for your home. Obtain your family’s approval. Show and discuss how you would use it in an emergency. Include the following in your kit:*
     - **2b1.** At least one of each of the following: flashlight with batteries, water (one gallon per person for minimum of 3 days), first-aid kit, nonperishable food (minimum of 3 days), three additional personal items
     - **2b2.** Discuss how you would adapt your kit for a dormitory, apartment, high-rise, or other special circumstance
   
   - *Practical tip:* Assemble a real kit (budget $20-50); test items like water purification. For 2b2, consider urban scenarios like no car access—add dust masks or cash.
   
   - **2c.** Tell how you and your family will communicate in the event of a disaster (e.g., evacuation).
   - *Practical tip:* Set family rally points and out-of-area contacts; practice a drill.

3. **Tell what you have learned from the American Red Cross document “Weapons of Mass Destruction: Guidance for the Family” or similar information from another reputable source.**
   - *Practical tip:* Download free from Red Cross site; focus on chemical/biological/radiological threats. Summarize key actions like shelter-in-place.

4. **Using the information from the “Be Prepared for Emergencies” brochure (or similar information from another reputable source), discuss the following:*
   - **4a.** How to use a fire extinguisher
   - **4b.** How to shut off utilities (water, electricity, gas)
   - **4c.** How to escape a burning building
   - **4d.** How to use a fire escape ladder (if available)
   - **4e.** How to assist someone needing help
   - *Practical tip:* Demo extinguisher (PASS method) at a station or school; practice valve shutoffs safely with adult supervision. Role-play building evacuation.

5. **Make a small display or prepare a presentation for your crew, team, or ship on what you have learned about preparedness for a specific natural disaster (e.g., tornado, hurricane, earthquake, etc.). Include at least five preparedness items that would be appropriate for that disaster.**
   - *Practical tip:* Choose a local hazard; create a poster or 5-minute talk with visuals. Present at a troop meeting—practice timing.

6. **Meet with a member of your crew, team, ship, or community who has been involved in emergency preparedness or response. Discuss with that person:**
   - **6a.** A past disaster or emergency in your community or home area
   - **6b.** What was accomplished by preparation before the disaster
   - **6c.** What was accomplished by response after the disaster
   - **6d.** What could have been done differently to improve preparation or response
   - *Practical tip:* Interview a firefighter/EMT via troop connections; record notes or video (with permission). Ask about real lessons like communication failures.

7. **Using the American Red Cross publication “Your Family Disaster Plan” or similar information from another reputable source, do the following:**
   - **7a.** Create a family emergency plan for your home.
   - **7b.** Have each member of your family read and sign the plan.
   - *Practical tip:* Customize for pets/kids/elderly; laminate and post it. Get signatures at dinner—makes it official.

8. **Plan and conduct a community or unit service project related to emergency preparedness. Examples include the following:**
   - **8a.** Distribute emergency preparedness materials
   - **8b.** Create a family emergency plan kit for a family in need
   - **8c.** Plan and conduct an emergency drill for your crew, team, ship, or community
   - **8d.** Work with a community agency or organization to prepare disaster kits for a special needs population (e.g., elderly, physically or mentally challenged)
   - *Practical tip:* Coordinate with SPL early; aim for 4-6 hours with 5+ participants. Document photos/log for badge app—easiest is a kit assembly event.[4]

9. **Do one of the following:**
   - **9a.** Using the Emergency Preparedness merit badge pamphlet, the American Red Cross “The Care of the Sick, Injured, and Dependent” brochure, or similar information from another reputable source, demonstrate first aid skills that would be helpful in an emergency. Include treatment for:* (1) choking * (2) shock * (3) bleeding * (4) heat stroke * (5) sunburn * (6) hypothermia * (7) sprains and strains * (8) fractures * (9) head injuries * (10) insect stings * (11) snakebites
   - **9b.** Visit with a first responder or emergency worker to learn about their role in an emergency. Ask questions about their training, equipment, and experiences. Share what you learned with your crew, team, ship, or patrol.
   - *Practical tip:* Choose 9a if First Aid is fresh; use dummies for demos. For 9b, tour a station and present findings.

**Overall tips:** Difficulty rated 5/10—focus on requirement 8 for leadership practice.[4] Get a counselor signature early; use the official workbook. Total time: 10-20 hours. For Eagle, pair with Camping for overlap. Always verify with current pamphlet as minor clarifications occur yearly.[7][10]
