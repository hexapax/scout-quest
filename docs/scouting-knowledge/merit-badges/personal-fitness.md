### Eagle-Required Status
**Personal Fitness** is an **Eagle-required merit badge** for Scouting America (formerly BSA), one of the 13 specific badges needed among the 21 total for Eagle Scout rank. Its status has not changed recently; it remains required as confirmed in official listings.[2][4]

### Current Requirements
The following are the **full, current requirements** for the Personal Fitness merit badge, numbered as officially stated (sourced from the merit badge pamphlet and authoritative Scouting sites). Scouts may encounter minor phrasing variations in older pamphlets (pre-2020s revisions emphasized the Annual Health and Medical Record form more explicitly), but core content remains consistent. All Scouts must start with a physical exam using the **Annual Health and Medical Record** form.[3][8]

1. **Do the following:**
   - (a) Before completing requirements 2 through 8, have your health-care practitioner give you a physical examination, using the **Scout medical examination form**. Explain the following:
     - (1) Why physical exams are important
     - (2) Why warm-up activities are important before exercise
     - (3) Why you should protect your feet when exercising
   - (b) Explain to your merit badge counselor **verbally or in writing** what **personal fitness** means to you, including:
     - (1) Reasons for being mentally, physically, socially, and spiritually fit[3][8]

   **Practical tips:** Schedule the physical early—use the free online Annual Health and Medical Record form from scouting.org. Discuss family health history with your doctor. For (b), write a 1-page essay tying fitness to Scouting values like preparedness; practice verbal delivery for confidence.[3][8]

2. **With your counselor, determine the best **fitness tests** for you and measure your current fitness in each of the four areas listed below—**aerobic fitness**, **muscular strength**, **muscular endurance**, and **flexibility**. Do **fitness tests every four weeks** (spread over at least 12 weeks) and record your results. Later compare your pre- and post-exercise measurements.[1][3]

   **Practical tips:** Use pamphlet guidelines: Aerobic (1-mile run/walk or 9-minute run/walk); Strength (push-ups, pull-ups, sit-ups—do sit-ups plus one other); Flexibility (sit-and-reach); Body comp (height/weight/BMI or skinfold). Track in a simple spreadsheet; retest consistently same time/day to avoid variables like fatigue.[1][3]

3. **Describe to your counselor the factors you should consider in designing a comprehensive fitness program.[3]

   **Practical tips:** Cover FITT (Frequency, Intensity, Time, Type), warm-ups, progression, rest days. Reference pamphlet diagrams for balanced routines avoiding injury.[3][7]

4. **Using the results of the tests from requirement 2, list your **three target fitness areas** (aerobic fitness, muscular strength/endurance, flexibility). For each of these fitness areas, discuss with your counselor how your present fitness level compares to one of the following:**
   - (a) Fitness rating charts in the Personal Fitness merit badge pamphlet
   - (b) Charts from fitness testing websites like President's Challenge or President's Council on Fitness, Sports & Nutrition
   - (c) Your counselor’s experience in fitness testing
   - (d) Other areas approved by your counselor[3]

   **Practical tips:** Pick weakest areas (e.g., if sit-ups score low). Use online calculators for age/gender norms; print charts for counselor sign-off.[1][3]

5. **Discuss with your counselor the following:**
   - (a) The benefits of good flexibility and how it is achieved and maintained
   - (b) The risks (legal, emotional, physical) of performance-enhancing drugs and supplements
   - (c) The need to warm up and cool down properly before and after exercise[3][8]

   **Practical tips:** Prep with pamphlet examples: stretches for flexibility, real athlete stories for drugs. Demo warm-up (jog + dynamic stretches) at counselor meeting.[3]

6. **Using the fitness tests in requirement 2, outline **personal goals** that meet the guidelines in the Personal Fitness merit badge pamphlet. Share your goals with your counselor.[3]

   **Practical tips:** Make SMART goals (e.g., "Improve 1-mile run by 2 minutes in 12 weeks"). Align with Tenderfoot/2nd Class/1st Class fitness for efficiency if under 15.[6]

7. **Outline a **comprehensive 12-week physical fitness program** using the results of the fitness tests from requirement 2. Be sure the program incorporates the **endurance, intensity, and warm-up guidelines** discussed in the Personal Fitness merit badge pamphlet. Before beginning your exercises, have the program approved by your counselor and your parents.[3][8]

   **Practical tips:** 3-5 days/week per area; vary exercises (e.g., switch push-ups to planks). Get written approval; use apps like MyFitnessPal for logging.[3][7]

8. **Complete the physical fitness program you outlined in requirement 7. Keep a log of your fitness program activity (how long you exercised; how far you ran, swam, or biked; how many exercise repetitions you completed; your exercise heart rate; etc.). Keep a log of your **weekly healthy eating goals**. Repeat the aerobic fitness, muscular strength, and flexibility tests every four weeks and record your results. After the 12th week, review your log with your counselor.[3][9]

   **Practical tips:** Log daily in notebook/app (include heart rate via phone/watch). Eat balanced: track 3 days first (req. 9 ties in). Adjust if plateauing—vary routines.[3][7]

9. **Keep track of what you eat and drink for **three days**. Identify **three healthy eating goals** you want to work on.[3]

   **Practical tips:** Use food diary app; aim for goals like "add veggies to every meal" or "cut soda." Discuss portion sizes, hydration with counselor.[3]

10. **Discuss your current **habits relating to nutrition, sleep, stress management, alcohol, tobacco, and drugs**. Explain to your counselor how you plan to maintain good habits.[3][8]

    **Practical tips:** Be honest—focus on positives/improvements. Tie to req. 1(b) for holistic view; set app reminders for sleep (8-10 hrs/night).[8]

11-12. **Answer the following (self-assessment questions from pamphlet):**
    - 11. Do you carry out daily activities without noticeable effort? Do you have extra energy for other activities?
    - 12. Are you free from habits relating to poor nutrition and the use of alcohol, tobacco, drugs, and other practices that could be harmful to your health?
    - (Note: Full list includes sleep, exercise participation—review pamphlet for exact 6-8 questions.)[3][8]

    **Practical tips:** Reflect journal-style; counselor verifies via discussion. Link to logs for evidence.[3]

### Additional Notes
- **Timeline:** Takes 12+ weeks—start early, especially for Eagle.[5][6][7]
- **Counselor Role:** Must approve program/goals; find registered via troop or scouting.org.[7]
- **Version Differences:** Post-2020 pamphlets standardize medical form and tests; older ones (pre-2016) had slight test options but same structure. Always use latest pamphlet.[3][8]
